\section{Aims of dissertation thesis}
\label{sec:aims}

The previous chapters established the technological foundations of blockchain and identified the Internet of Value as an emerging paradigm that enables direct, peer-to-peer exchange of heterogeneous assets through decentralised infrastructure. The state of the art makes assets representable, transferable, and settleable, but it does not provide a formal layer for interpreting what those assets are worth under a stated user or governance intent before any value-relevant action is authorised.

Our analysis identified three critical gaps in the current state of the art. First, asset valuation in the Internet of Value is repeatedly reduced to market price, even though the value of many on-chain assets lies in utility, governance weight, access rights, contribution to a public good, or productive yield rather than in floor price \cite{Lohmer2024, Ante2021}. Second, existing blockchain account models, including recent account-abstraction proposals such as EIP-4337 \cite{Buterin2021, Wang2023, Duan2024}, are organised around flexible authentication, gas sponsorship, and signing delegation rather than around account-level enforcement of intent-conditioned policy and audit-trace gating. Third, on-chain treasury governance assumes that human voters can evaluate proposals at the rate at which they arrive, an assumption that breaks once treasury volume scales \cite{Valastin2024proto}. These three gaps share a common cause. The infrastructure provides settlement primitives and execution primitives, but it lacks a decision layer that makes intent-conditioned value interpretation auditable before action.

The maturation of large language models and structured tool-orchestration interfaces such as the Model Context Protocol makes it technically feasible to delegate parts of evidence retrieval and structured analysis to autonomous agents \cite{Sumers2024, Wu2023}. End-to-end determinism still depends on tool behaviour and the execution environment, which is why explicit policy boundaries and trace-gated authorisation in the on-chain layer remain necessary. The question this thesis takes up is therefore not whether such delegation is possible. It is on what formal terms agentic recommendations about value can be made auditable, evidence-complete, and policy-constrained before they translate into on-chain actions.

The central contribution of this dissertation is \emph{Intent-Conditioned Value Projection (ICVP)}. ICVP is a formal decision model in which stated user or governance intent acts as a projection over heterogeneous Internet of Value evidence spaces. Intent does not only change how much Availability, Durability, and Utility matter. It changes what counts as Availability, Durability, and Utility in the first place. For previously unseen asset classes, ICVP does not require predefined asset-specific value formulas. It requires an intent-specific projection over available evidence, after which Availability, Durability, and Utility are instantiated for the asset class and action context. The output of ICVP is a valuation trace that records the stated intent, the selected evidence, the intent-instantiated dimensions, the decision in $\{\textit{proceed}, \textit{defer}, \textit{decline}\}$, and the provenance needed to audit the recommendation.

The research questions of this thesis are:

\begin{enumerate}
   \item \textbf{RQ1.} What formal model can represent stated user or governance intent as a projection over heterogeneous Internet of Value evidence spaces, producing intent-instantiated valuation dimensions and auditable decision signals for assets and asset-related actions?
   \item \textbf{RQ2.} What method can operationalise Intent-Conditioned Value Projection through auditable valuation traces in an agentic decision-support system while preserving evidence traceability, human approval, and policy-constrained transaction preparation?
   \item \textbf{RQ3.} To what extent does Intent-Conditioned Value Projection improve decision quality, evidence relevance, and auditability across NFT, productive-asset, and treasury-governance scenarios compared with single-dimensional and fixed-weight valuation baselines?
\end{enumerate}

These three questions correspond to three contribution pillars. RQ1 asks for a formal model. The answer is the ICVP formalisation of intent as evidence projection, of Availability, Durability, and Utility as intent-instantiated valuation dimensions, and of the auditable valuation trace as a first-class artefact rather than a narrative explanation. RQ2 asks for a method that puts this model to work. The answer is an agentic decision-support pipeline in which valuation, decision, and execution components retrieve evidence, propose intent-conditioned valuation traces, validate them deterministically, route them through human approval, and prepare policy-constrained transactions for an account-level enforcement primitive. The Non-Fungible Token as Account (NFTAA) primitive proposed in this work plays the role of that enforcement primitive. NFTAA does not compute value. It enforces that only human-approved, policy-compliant actions linked to a valid valuation trace can affect assets on-chain. RQ3 asks for empirical evidence. The answer is a framework-validation evaluation across twelve tasks that span Pudgy Penguin NFT or access decisions, stETH productive-asset decisions, and Polkadot OpenGov treasury-governance decisions, comparing the full ICVP model against a single-dimensional baseline that collapses each decision to its dominant visible signal and a fixed-weight baseline that keeps Availability, Durability, and Utility but removes intent-conditioned projection and dimension instantiation.

The thesis does not claim a universal theory of value, that the underlying language model is correct, that experts are absolute ground truth, or that agents should autonomously control user assets. It claims that stated intent can be formalised as a projection over heterogeneous evidence spaces, that intent-instantiated Availability, Durability, and Utility provide a practical decomposition of value across heterogeneous Internet of Value asset classes, that valuation traces can make agentic decision support auditable, that confident \textit{proceed} decisions can be gated by evidence completeness, and that asset-moving authorisation can be gated by trace validity, human approval, and account-level policy. To make the formal scope of this claim precise, the thesis targets two safety properties. \emph{Evidence-Completeness Safety} states that a confident \textit{proceed} decision implies the presence and provenance of the intent-required evidence. \emph{Trace-Gated Authorisation} states that authorisation of a value-relevant transaction implies the existence of a valid trace, complete intent-required evidence, a \textit{proceed} decision, a transaction that matches the trace, and a permitting policy. Throughout the thesis we use \emph{auditable}, \emph{evidence-based}, \emph{intent-conditioned}, \emph{traceable}, \emph{attributable}, and \emph{policy-constrained} in preference to the term \emph{objective}, which we treat as misleading in this setting.

Along with the main research questions, we have identified specific challenges that correspond to each research area:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item There is no framework that can objectively evaluate heterogeneous asset types while adapting to diverse user intents.
  \item Current blockchain systems lack a standard architecture for integrating autonomous agents with on-chain execution in a modular, extensible manner.
  \item Manual evaluation of on-chain treasury proposals is biased and does not scale, resulting in inconsistent decision-making.
\end{enumerate}

The following chapter develops the design that addresses these challenges. Chapter \ref{sec:design} formalises ICVP, presents the agentic method that carries it out, and reframes NFTAA as the account-level enforcement layer that makes trace-gated authorisation concrete. Chapter \ref{sec:evaluation} reports the twelve-task evaluation against the two reduced baselines and the three expert reference judgements.
