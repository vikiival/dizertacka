\section{Design}
\label{sec:design}

This chapter introduces key concepts to enhance the understanding and technological framework of the Internet of Value (IoV). We propose a new architectural approach to increase efficiency in asset sharing within blockchain ecosystems using agentic systems. This approach is in accordance with our targets discussed in Chapter \ref{sec:aims}.
While our evaluation targets EVM-compatible environments and Polkadot, the architectural concepts presented here are designed to be blockchain-agnostic and can be adapted to other ecosystems such as Solana or Cosmos. As the Internet of Value is a broad concept, our domain knowledge focuses on AI agents, value creation, and sharing.

\subsection{Asset valuation using agentic systems}

Value is a concept that is difficult to define in general terms, as it is subjective and context-dependent. As we mentioned in the Chapter \ref{sec:aims}, Lohmer et al. \cite{Lohmer2024} developed a comprehensive definition and framework of IoV through expert interviews. From their research, they concluded on a multidimensional notion of value that encompasses economic worth, utility or access rights, and Ethical and sustainability contributions.

Based on these propositions, we have defined value as a function of availability, durability, and utility.

Therefore, we define the value $V$ of an asset $a$ as a weighted function of three independent components:

\begin{equation}
V(a) = w_A \cdot A(a) + w_D \cdot D(a) + w_U \cdot U(a)
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $A(a) \in [0, 10]$ represents the availability (scarcity) of the asset
    \item $D(a) \in [0, 10]$ represents the durability (resilience and longevity) of the asset
    \item $U(a) \in [0, 10]$ represents the utility (functional benefits) of the asset
    \item $w_A, w_D, w_U \in [0, 1]$ are intent-dependent weights satisfying $w_A + w_D + w_U = 1$
\end{itemize}

Each component is normalized to a 10-point scale to provide interpretable scores that can be compared across heterogeneous asset types. The weights are dynamically adjusted based on user intent, which we formalize later in the section.

In terms of availability, $A(a)$ quantifies how abundant or scarce an asset is in its environment. The circulating supply, maximum supply, and inflationary or deflationary processes all affect the availability of fungible tokens. The size of the entire collection, the rarity of certain characteristics, and the presence of processes like minting or burning capabilities that might change future supply all affect the availability of non-fungible tokens.

The second component of value is durability $D(a)$. The durability of an asset is determined by its longevity and resilience. The longevity of an asset is the length of time it remains in existence. The resilience of an asset is its ability to withstand shocks and stresses.
Unlike physical assets, where durability depends on material quality and environmental conditions, the durability of a digital asset is determined by three factors: metadata storage guarantees, smart contract immutability, and ecosystem resilience.

Durability is critical for long-term holding, institutional custody, or any scenario where asset integrity over time is essential. An NFT with high durability can be confidently used as collateral, transferred across generations, or integrated into legal contracts, as its properties are guaranteed to remain stable.

So what is the utility of an asset? The utility $U(a)$ of an asset captures the functional benefits an asset provides to its holder.
Unlike availability and durability, which have relatively objective measurement criteria, utility is inherently subjective and context-dependent.

For non-fungible tokens, utility ranges from aesthetic enjoyment (digital art) to functional access (event tickets, membership passes) to productive use (in-game items, intellectual property licenses).

However, utility is highly intent-dependent. A social participant may find great use for an NFT that acts as a key to an exclusive community, whereas a merely speculative investor may find it less useful. On the other hand, an in-game asset could be very useful to a player but insignificant to a non-gamer.

Therefore, we return to the last part of our value definition: intent-dependent weights.
The weights $w_A, w_D, and w_U$ are dynamically allocated according to the user's objectives, whether it collateralization, long-term holding, speculative investment, or functional use. The valuation framework may accommodate a variety of user objectives while preserving a uniform assessment structure by modifying these weights.

whether it's for practical usage, long-term keeping, speculative investment, or collateralization. By altering these weights, the valuation framework may support a range of user goals while maintaining a consistent evaluation structure.


How can an agentic system help us determine the value of an asset based on the token's intrinsic properties? That is a fundamental question. Since agents need proper context to evaluate criteria, the base of our architecture starts with Contextual Asset Value, where we define the properties of non-fungible token.

\subsection{Methodology proposal}

The methodology consists of four different parts that transform user intent into verifiable on-chain actions. A distinct pipeline from intent to settlement is created by mapping each phase to distinct architectural layers. The methodology is based on the previously established top value framework, which guarantees that the concepts of availability, durability, and usefulness are translated into tangible behavior.

Therefore, we propose the following setup for our methodology for asset valorization in agentic systems within the Internet of Value:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data Acquisition:} Based on the intent, the AI agent initiates requests to access external data sources. This data includes information necessary for evaluating NFTs, such as metadata, market trends, and usage statistics with respect to Availability, Durability and Utility.
    \item \textbf{Contextual Analysis:} AI agents evaluate the data in order to determine the value of the NFT. This evaluation takes into account elements such as functional benefits (utility), lifespan and robustness (durability), and scarcity (availability).
    \item \textbf{Intent-based Decision Making:} AI agents use the analysis to make well-informed judgments about the NFT, such as suggesting transactions, updating information, or initiating smart contract operations.
    \item \textbf{Agent based execution:}  The decisions made by AI agents are executed on the blockchain through smart contracts, ensuring transparency, immutability, and decentralized governance.
\end{enumerate}


In the following subsection, we will describe the technical and operational requirements for a functional architecture for agentic systems in the context of the Internet of Value and NFTs. Requirements are organized systematically, into four categories: data acquisition, agent capabilities, execution guarantees, and security properties.

\subsection{System Requirements}


To compute the value components $A(a)$, $D(a)$, and $U(a)$ for an arbitrary asset $a$, the system must acquire heterogeneous data from multiple sources. These requirements ensure that agents have access to information. Therefore, we define the following data acquisition requirements:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The system should retrieve data from at least one data source, namely: Blockchain explorers, Decentralized storage networks, Marketplace APIs, Rarity analytics, and Oracle services.
    \item Data from heterogeneous sources must be normalized into a canonical schema that enables cross-asset comparison. For example, metadata from OpenSea, LooksRare, and native IPFS retrieval must be transformed into a standard JSON structure with standardized field names.
    \item Each data point should be accompanied by provenance information: the source URL, retrieval timestamp, and content hash. This ensures verifiability and auditability of the acquired information.
\end{itemize}

The agentic system must possess specific capabilities to analyze assets and make decisions based on user intent. These capabilities include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item System should accept user intent expressed in natural language.
    \item System should translate user intent into formal weight assignments $w_A, w_D, w_U$ .
    \item Given an asset identifier (contract address and token ID) and intent weights, the valuation agent should return score for $A(a)$, $D(a)$, and $U(a)$ and along with a structured report with justifications for each score.
    \item The decision agent must produce a structured recommendation in a JSON format.
    \item The execution agent should enforce on-chain policies before submitting transactions. These policies include: Spending limits,Allowed protocols, value verification requirements\footnote{e.g. minimum durability score}.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Architecture}

The proposed architecture connects human intent with blockchain settlement through three tightly integrated layers. Through the local layer, participants may articulate their objectives in regular language, which is subsequently verified and transformed into standardized prompts. In the agentic layer, these prompts are processed by autonomous agents that evaluate assets along the availability, durability, and utility axes, interpret the findings according to purpose, and generate structured suggestions. When these choices are approved, execution agents convert them into on-chain transactions, which are then upheld by smart contracts at the on-chain layer. By closing the loop between cognition and settlement, this layered approach guarantees visible, repeatable, and secure operations while maintaining the flexibility to eventually integrate additional data sources and agent capabilities.

    \label{fig:architecture}

In Figure \ref{fig:architecture}, we present the proposed architecture for agentic systems in the context of the Internet of Value. The system is composed of four parts: the Data layer, the Agentic layer, the Local layer, and the Onchain layer. At the bottom, the on-chain layer anchors the system through smart contracts that implement token standards, access control, and event emission. These contracts act as an interface for an agentic system, ensuring that every transaction is transparent and verifiable.

The agentic layer is a semi-autonomous layer of AI bots that may interact with users and carry out on-chain smart contracts. In this layer, valuation agents gather asset data from dispersed sources including price feeds, IPFS, marketplaces, and rarity tools. While execution agents connect directly with the blockchain to carry out the authorized operations, decision agents convert these results into structured suggestions that are modified based on user intent. Model Context Protocol adapters allow the agentic layer to interact with external data sources, guaranteeing consistent, repeatable tool invocation. Lastly, the entrance point for human players is formed by the local layer. Before reaching the agentic layer, intent is first stated in natural language, verified, and converted into standardized prompts. By structuring the system in this way, the architecture closes the loop between human intention, autonomous reasoning, and decentralized settlement, while maintaining security, transparency, and extensibility.

The data layer's inclusion highlights the need for trustworthy information streams to be the foundation of any effective digital asset appraisal. This layer collects and standardizes data from many sources, including as public block explorers, decentralized storage systems like IPFS, centralized and decentralized marketplaces, rarity ranking services, and external price feeds oracles. The primary role of the data layer is to provide structured, machine-readable input to the agentic layer, abstracting away differences between providers while preserving verifiability of origin. In this sense, the data layer functions as the knowledge substrate upon which agents perform their contextual analysis.

The interaction between these four layers follows a continuous cycle. The process starts with the user formulating their intent at the local layer. The intent is verified, recorded, and converted into a uniform prompt. After receiving the message, the agentic layer arranges the work of specialized agents. Valuation agents evaluate availability, durability, and utility by querying the data layer to obtain asset-specific information. After that, decision agents calculate a context-dependent value function and generate a structured suggestion by combining these evaluations with the user's stated goal.

By organizing the architecture in this manner, we achieve a modular yet tightly coupled system. Each layer has a clearly defined role: the on-chain layer ensures settlement and immutability, the data layer offers reliable information, the agentic layer facilitates cognition and decision-making, and the local layer mediates human contact. This division of responsibilities guarantees extensibility and strengthens the overall design's resilience. The integrity of the system may be maintained when integrating new data sources, agent kinds, or smart contract modules. Moreover, reliance on open token standards and decentralized storage solutions ensures interoperability across EVM-based and non-EVM chains. At the same time, the use of MCP secures the predictable behavior of autonomous agents. Together, these properties create an architecture that is transparent, secure, and adaptable.


\subsection{Agentic Layer}

Without any doubt, the agentic layer is a core component of our architecture. It mediates between human intent, raw blockchain data, and the on-chain settlement layer, enabling intelligent and context-aware interactions with digital assets. Unlike the on-chain layer, which is inherently deterministic and immutable, the agentic layer introduces flexibility, reasoning, and adaptation through autonomous AI processes. Execution agents operationalize validated decisions on-chain, valuation agents convert heterogeneous data into structured metrics, decision agents contextualize these metrics based on user intent, and MCP adapters guarantee consistent and repeatable communication with external tools. Together, these components create a closed loop that begins with user intent and results in verifiable blockchain state changes.

\begin{figure}[!h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{images/agentic-sequence.png}
    \caption{Flow from user to Valuation Agent}
    \label{fig:agentic-sequence}

The valuation agent is responsible for the contextual analysis of assets within the Internet of Value. As seen in Figure \ref{fig:agentic-sequence}, its main responsibility is to calculate the three aspects of value: usefulness, durability, and availability. In order to accomplish this, the agent continually gathers information from a variety of sources, such as markets, decentralized storage networks (like IPFS), blockchain explorers, rarity analytics platforms, and oracle services. The agent normalizes this information into a consistent internal representation, which allows it to compare diverse assets across collections and chains.

In the context of NFTs, for availability the valuation agent determines scarcity by inspecting the overall token supply and trait distribution.
We compute both, how many tokens exist relative to the maximum possible supply (absolute scarcity) and how specific token ranks within the collection based on its trait combination (rarity).
Durability is evaluated by two main sub-components: storage reliability, mutability risk, and optionally ecosystem resilience. Storage reliability depends on where the metadata of the particular token resides. Clearly, on-chain storage has the greatest score, IPFS with many pinning services get a somewhat lower grade, and centralized servers like S3 receive the lowest. By examining the smart contract for potentially harmful functions like \texttt{setBaseURI} that could change metadata pointers, determining whether contract ownership is renounced or controlled by a multi-signature wallet, and confirming whether the upgrade authority has been revoked on chains like Solana, mutability risk is evaluated. Optionally, ecosystem resilience considers the security and stability of the underlying blockchain, factoring in validator decentralization, historical uptime, and resistance to consensus attacks.
Utility is evaluated by looking at the asset's current and future use cases. The agent recognizes current features such community access rights, in-game usability, and involvement in governance. In order to find planned enhancements that might eventually improve the asset's usefulness, it also assesses potential utility by examining roadmaps, development activity, and community involvement. Moreover, utility is vital for intents where usefulness is prioritized over scarcity or durability (e.g., Does this NFT grant DAO governance rights?).

The valuation agent produces a structured report that details the computed scores for availability, durability, and utility, along with justifications for each score. This report serves as the foundational input for the decision agent, which will contextualize these metrics according to user intent. By modularizing the valuation process in this way, the architecture ensures that raw data is transformed into meaningful insights that can inform higher-level reasoning and decision-making.

How can a large language model become an expert in asset valorization? That's an important question. It starts with a system prompt, which can be imagined as a set of permanent instructions for how the AI model should behave. In Listing \ref{lst:systemprompt}, we can see an excerpt from the system prompt for the valuation agent.

Once we have output, we can pass it to the decision agent. However, thanks to our modular architecture, we can plug and play any number of models in the pipeline.

\subsubsection{Decision Agent}

The decision agent transforms analytical insights into actionable recommendations. While the valuation agent provides a factual description of an asset's characteristics, the decision agent incorporates user intent into its reasoning. Intent may range from speculative investment and long-term holding to immediate consumption or resale. The decision agent's analysis is represented as structured JSON output, as seen in Listing \ref{lst:structuredoutput}. The decision agent creates a quantitative value score by using a weighted function that integrates availability, durability, and usefulness with the contextualized purpose. This score is supported with a confidence measure and qualitative rationale to guarantee that the results are auditable and interpretable. Additionally, the decision agent takes into account edge circumstances, including excessive volatility or missing data, and modifies its confidence appropriately.

How are the weights determined? The weights are obtained from pre-established intent profiles that associate particular weight distributions with typical user objectives. For instance, a long-term holder would place more emphasis on durability, but a speculative investor might value availability and usefulness. Based on the user's stated purpose, the decision agent determines the proper weight set and adds it to the valuation agent's scores. We define several canonical intent profiles:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Speculative Investment}: Maximize short-term appreciation potential
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $w_A = 0.6, w_D = 0.1, w_U = 0.3$
        \item Prioritizes scarcity and momentum-driven utility; durability matters less for quick flips.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Long-Term Holding}: Maximize preservation of value over time
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $w_A = 0.2, w_D = 0.6, w_U = 0.2$
        \item Prioritizes durability to ensure the asset remains intact and recognizable decades later.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Functional Use}: Maximize immediate practical benefits
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $w_A = 0.1, w_D = 0.2, w_U = 0.7$
        \item Prioritizes current utility for access, governance, or consumption; scarcity and durability are secondary.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Collateralization}: Balance safety and utility for DeFi integration
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $w_A = 0.3, w_D = 0.5, w_U = 0.2$
        \item Requires high durability for lender confidence and moderate availability for liquidation feasibility.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}


This dynamic weighting allows the system to adapt its recommendations to diverse user needs while maintaining a consistent evaluation framework.

The decision agent outputs its recommendation in structured JSON fromat, which guarantees compatibility with downstream tools and ensures reusability with other systems: agent consistently produces the same structured response.

The execution agent is in charge of putting the decision agent's suggestions into practice. The execution agent safely communicates with the blockchain once an activity, such buying an NFT, transferring a token, or engaging with a governance contract, is verified by a human-in-the-loop procedure. Since the agent knows the parameters of the inspected data, such as the smart contract address, the account address, or the token ID, we can submit a valid on-chain transaction along with its simulation. The execution agent manages cryptographic signing, nonce management, and gas optimization, thereby shielding the user from low-level technical complexities. It also monitors the blockchain for confirmations and provides verifiable proofs of execution back to the user, such as the transaction hash. To enhance resilience, the execution agent is designed with fail-safe strategies. In cases of failed execution, it will notify the user of the cause. The transparency of its actions is guaranteed by on-chain settlement, which produces immutable records of every decision.

\subsection{On-chain Layer}

The on-chain layer constitutes the settlement and trust foundation of the proposed architecture. It is here that all agentic decisions are ultimately anchored and enforced through blockchain primitives. Unlike the agentic layer, which is adaptive and reasoning-oriented, the on-chain layer provides determinism, immutability, and verifiability, ensuring that every executed action is transparently recorded in the distributed ledger.

Agent-based execution begins when a decision execution agent receives an approval from the human-in-the-loop process, which initiates a multi-step safety verification process before submitting any transaction to the blockchain.

At the core of this layer lies the \textbf{Non-fungible Token as Account (NFTAA)} concept, as introduced by Valastin et al. \cite{Valastin2024nftaa}.
NFTAA expands the conventional ERC-721 model to allow an asset to be owned and to own other assets, whereas conventional NFTs only exist on the asset side of the owner-asset relationship. This creates a bidirectional ownership model that is crucial for autonomous agent operation within the Internet of Value. Non-fungible assets and accounts are two previously distinct blockchain primitives that are connected by this basic innovation.

According to this paradigm, NFTs may store balances, carry out transactions, and communicate directly with other smart contracts, making them active members of the blockchain ecosystem. In the context of the Internet of Value, this abstraction is very potent, allowing NFTs to function as execution agents, value transporters, and identity containers. By embedding account functionality into NFTs, the architecture gains greater composability and interoperability with decentralized applications.

One of the advantages of NFTAA is the ability to operate and own standard fungible and non-fungible assets. This implies that assets stored in an agent-controlled NFTAA can engage in yield farming, lending, borrowing, and derivative markets without needing to be withdrawn. While keeping a distance from the user's primary wallet and facilitating transparent, auditable agent conduct on-chain, an agent overseeing a portfolio of NFTs within an NFTAA can offer liquidity, stake governance tokens, or collateralize them on lending protocols.

Furthermore, smart contracts ensure that only authorized execution agents can initiate critical state changes by defining access control rules and enforcing invariants. NFTAA offers a number of crucial features that set it apart from current token-bound account standards, especially ERC-6551\cite{Wang2023}, when compared to alternatives like Virtuals that employ ERC-6551.
First, instead of depending on a strict, registry-based deployment architecture, NFTAA enables the integration of specific semantic rules directly into the token contract. ERC-6551 limits the design space for specific behaviors by deploying each token-bound account via a singleton registry with a deterministic address scheme. By enabling developers to create custom account logic inside the NFT contract itself, NFTAA eliminates this restriction and provides fine-grained control over state transitions, access patterns, and execution policies.

Second, NFTAA preserves complete compatibility with current account types. NFTAA can easily communicate with multi-signature accounts, smart contract wallets, proxy contracts, and externally owned accounts (EOAs), in contrast to alternatives that need specific wallet infrastructure. In the context of the Internet of Value, where agents must coordinate with a variety of actors, such as human users, other agents, and smart contract protocols, this interoperability is essential.

Third, NFTAA enables non-custodial ownership of complex asset portfolios. In traditional liquid staking or asset management scenarios, users must delegate control to DAOs, multi-signature schemes, or centralized entities. The NFT holder maintains complete custody while the NFTAA contract handles staked positions, derivative instruments, or collateralized assets on their behalf, therefore eliminating this middleman. This means that in agentic systems, users retain sovereign authority by owning the parent NFT, but execution agents can function independently without the need for reliable third parties.

What is the behavior of the NFTAA in agentic systems? The operational needs of autonomous agents in the Internet of Value reveal the alignment between NFTAA and agentic architectures.
The NFT represents both the agent's identity and its resource container, thus NFTAA may be thought of as an agent-controlled but user-owned account.
The execution agent does not work from a shared, multipurpose wallet when a valuation agent evaluates an asset, a decision agent suggests a transaction, and a human authorizes it. Rather, it uses a specific NFTAA instance to function. This encapsulation offers a number of advantages:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Isolation and Security:} Each agent operates within its own NFTAA context, isolating its actions and state from other agents. This containment reduces the risk of cross-agent interference or exploitation, enhancing overall system security.
    \item \textbf{Traceability and Accountability:} All actions taken by the agent are recorded under the NFTAA's address, creating a clear audit trail that links decisions to specific agent identities. This transparency is vital for trust in autonomous systems.
    \item \textbf{Composability and Flexibility:} NFTAA's compatibility with existing token standards allows agents to interact with a wide range of assets and protocols without requiring specialized infrastructure. Agents can seamlessly manage fungible tokens, stake assets, or engage in DeFi activities as needed.
\end{itemize}

In the context of our architecture, NFTAA serves as the settlement primitive for agent-mediated transactions. The agentic layer uses valuation and decision agents to handle user intent expressed through the local layer. However, an NFTAA under the execution agent's authority is used to carry out the actual execution. Additional control policies are set up in the NFTAA contract. These policies make sure that the agent's activities stay in line with user intent and system standards by enforcing restrictions like expenditure caps, permitted protocols, and value verification requirements. This architecture guarantees that the on-chain NFTAA contract operates as a cryptoeconomic guardrail, refusing to execute transactions that break pre-committed policies, even in the event that an execution agent is corrupted or exhibits unexpected behavior.

From an implementation perspective, NFTAA leverages standard NFT implementations\footnote{e.g., ERC-721, ERC-1155} as its base, extending them with account-like functionality. This includes methods for executing transactions, managing balances, and enforcing access control. The NFTAA contract can be deployed on any EVM-compatible chain, ensuring broad applicability across the Internet of Value ecosystem. Additionally, NFTAA instances can be dynamically created by the agentic layer as new agents are instantiated, enabling scalable deployment of autonomous agents without manual intervention. Additionally, the NFTAA contract can be expanded to comply with the emerging ERC-8004 standard for Trustless Agents.

Due to its flexible nature, NFTAA can be owned by various entities, including individuals, DAOs, or even other agents. This versatility allows for complex ownership structures, such as multi-agent collaborations or hierarchical control schemes, where multiple agents coordinate actions under a shared NFTAA umbrella.

However, how do we trigger the execution from the agentic layer to the on-chain layer? The process begins in the local layer when a user expresses their goal through the user interface, which will be introduced in the next section.

\subsection{Local Layer}

As the architecture's human-agent interface, the local layer gives end users the ability to communicate intent, keep control, and preserve sovereignty over system operations. The local layer prioritizes usability, accountability, and security, whereas the agentic and on-chain layers deal with cognition and settlement. The prompt manager, authentication and authorization procedures, and user interface make up its three main parts.

Human participants can access the Internet of Value through the user interface. Its function is to abstract the technical intricacies of blockchain interactions and agentic reasoning, allowing users to express their meaning in either a simpler, organized form or in normal language. Rather of needing to understand contract addresses, token standards, or transaction parameters, the user may make simple requests like "buy this token if it falls below ten ether" or "evaluate the value of NFT \#1419." Such phrases are converted by the interface into a standardized representation that may be used by subsequent operations. The interface promotes inclusiveness and lowers the possibility of mistakes in manual transaction creation by decreasing the entrance barrier and increasing involvement in decentralized economies. In order to keep the user informed about the logic and actions of the system, the interface also offers visible feedback in the form of audit trails, confidence scores, and structured suggestions.


Strong authentication and permission procedures are essential at the local layer due to the sensitivity of carrying out blockchain transactions. By using cryptographic wallets, decentralized identity frameworks, or multi-factor authentication techniques, authentication makes sure that only authorized users may access the system. By specifying what a certain user is allowed to do, authorization expands this protection. For example, unless more verification is given, a user could be permitted to seek asset appraisals but not to carry out token transfers. This division of authorization and authentication minimizes attack surfaces while maintaining usability in accordance with least privilege principles. Since all authorized activities can be cryptographically linked to the starting user, the system also provides non-repudiation, which is essential for accountability in a decentralized setting.

The task of converting human intent into deterministic machine-readable instructions falls to the prompt manager. This element is essential for bridging the gap between predictable agent behavior and natural language input. It converts the user's request into a prompt that complies with the Model Context Protocol (MCP) after verifying it against the system's schema. This ensures that when the same prompt is provided to the agentic layer, it always produces the same sequence of tool invocations and responses. Additionally, the prompt manager incorporates contextual metadata into the created prompt, including user intent, previous interaction history, and execution limitations. Reproducibility and accountability are supported by keeping an audit trail of all prompts and their associated outputs, which enables external reviewers to confirm that actions were carried out accurately. Furthermore, the prompt manager prevents the agentic layer from being pushed into dangerous or inadvertent actions by rejecting requests that are faulty or illegal.

\subsection{Usage of Agents in on-chain governance}

In traditional blockchain-based governance systems, human actors (token holders, delegates, and DAO committees) propose, vote, and execute governance decisions. These processes rely heavily on manual intervention, periodic coordination, and human diligence. As governance complexity increases (across multi-chain protocols, cross-project dependencies, and frequent parameter upgrades), this manual model becomes a bottleneck. Agentic systems, when integrated into the governance plane, promise to automate, optimize, and scale governance in a trust-minimized and auditable way.

In this sub-section, we will expand on a real-world use case of using our state-of-the-art architecture for exchanging assets among entities in the Internet of Value, with a primary focus on Polkadot.
At the time of writing, the Polkadot treasury holds more than 10.8 million DOT\footnote{Around ~43 million USD}. That could be distributed among people. However, many people claim that the proposal's pricing is unjust; therefore, the outflow of funds and its overall value seem mispriced.
An agentic system could be used to evaluate the proposals based on the contextual asset value framework. Therefore, each proposal would be evaluated based on its availability, durability, and utility. The decision agent would then analyze the data and recommend whether to approve or reject the proposal. The execution agent would then execute the decision on-chain.

In practice, how does it operate? Assume that the Polkadot Treasury receives a fresh proposal. The Submitted event on the Referenda pallet\footnote{https://polkadot.subscan.io/event?module=referenda&event_id=Submitted} creates an agent using a blockchain-in-the-loop method.The proposal's budget, schedule, and anticipated results would then be gathered by the valuation agency. After analyzing the information, the decision agent would suggest whether or not to accept the proposition. Execute the decision on-chain when it has been approved by a human.

This approach has several benefits. Firstly, it would ensure that proposals are evaluated based on objective criteria, rather than subjective opinions. Secondly, it would free up human actors to concentrate on more strategic duties by lessening their burden. Lastly, because the final judgment is recorded on-chain, it would improve the governance process's accountability and transparency.

This use case demonstrates how agentic systems might improve blockchain governance by automating decision-making procedures while upholding transparency and trust. By leveraging the strengths of both human judgment and autonomous reasoning, such systems can help decentralized communities manage resources more effectively in an increasingly complex ecosystem.

This creates a strong loop between human intent, autonomous reasoning, and decentralized settlement, enabling more efficient and scalable governance in the Internet of Value.

However, many open questions and challenges remain to be addressed. For example, how can we ensure the agents align with the community's values and goals? How to prevent manipulation or gaming of the system? How to balance automation with human oversight? These are all valid questions that we will explain in the following paragraphs.
As we have learned so far, the decision agent produces structured output that includes a confidence score and reasoning. This allows human actors to review the agent's recommendations and make informed decisions. Additionally, an agent can consider similar proposals from the past when making recommendations. The agent may learn from prior achievements and avoid making the same mistakes thanks to this historical background. A local layer can be used to offer further context about the proposal, such as a voting suggestion from W3F\footnote{https://medium.com/web3foundation/w3f-opengov-proposal-voting-guidelines-543ff7faba03}.



\subsection{Advancing Beyond the State of the Art}
%  \label{sec:advancing-sota}

We have addressed the basic problem of linking human intent with on-chain execution by presenting a methodical methodology for integrating agentic technologies with the Internet of Value. The architecture is based on three pillars that together allow asset appraisal and transaction execution to be autonomous, repeatable, and verifiable.

Formalizing asset value as a function of availability, durability, and utility is the first pillar. We give agents solid context that acts as an objective foundation for decision-making by basing value evaluation on quantifiable on-chain and off-chain attributes rather than subjective judgment. This system maintains interpretability and auditability while adapting to a variety of user purposes, from long-term preservation to speculative trading, thanks to the intent-dependent weighting mechanism.

The second pillar is the modular agentic architecture that decomposes cognition into specialized components: valuation, decision, and execution agents. Because each agent concentrates on a distinct aspect of the reasoning process, issues can be clearly separated, debugging is made simpler, and autonomous growth is possible. The decision agent contextualizes these metrics based on user intent, the execution agent operationalizes authorized activities on-chain, and the valuation agent specializes in data collection and metric calculation. As the ecosystem develops, plug-and-play integration of additional models or data sources is made possible by this modularity, which also improves maintainability.

The third pillar and key innovation of this design is the use of Non-Fungible Token as Account (NFTAA) as the execution primitive. NFTAA allows for the direct embedding of custom semantic rules into token contracts, in contrast to token-bound accounts like ERC-6551 that depend on strict registry-based deployment. With programmable limits, risk isolation, and on-chain policy enforcement, this flexibility enables agents to function as autonomous economic entities. Without needing off-chain trust assumptions, an NFTAA may concurrently represent agent identification, act as a resource container for managed assets, and impose spending limitations, metadata verification requirements, and protocol allowlists.

Additionally, the design highlights a number of unresolved issues that need more research. Currently, ethical and sustainable aspects are not included in the value framework, despite the fact that they could be crucial for particular communities or legal situations. Instead of being learnt from user behavior, the weights in intent profiles are intentionally set, which presents a chance for machine learning optimization. Although the security model makes the assumption that MCP adapters and data sources are reliable, a thorough threat model has to take into account hostile data providers, hacked API endpoints, and large-scale quick injection attacks.

We established the theoretical foundation and architectural blueprint for trustworthy agentic systems in the Internet of Value. By formalizing value, deriving systematic requirements, and designing a layered architecture with clear separation of concerns, we provide a pathway toward autonomous agents that can evaluate assets, make context-aware decisions, and execute transactions with the transparency, security, and user sovereignty that decentralized ecosystems demand.
