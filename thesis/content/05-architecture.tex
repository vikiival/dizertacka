\section{Design}
\label{sec:design}

This chapter introduces key concepts to enhance the understanding and technological framework of the Internet of Value (IoV). We propose a new architectural approach to increase efficiency in asset sharing within blockchain ecosystems using agentic systems. This approach is in accordance with our targets discussed in Chapter \ref{sec:aims}.
While our evaluation targets EVM-compatible environments and Polkadot, the architectural concepts presented here are designed to be blockchain-agnostic and can be adapted to other ecosystems such as Solana or Cosmos. As the Internet of Value is a broad concept, our domain knowledge focuses on AI agents, value creation, and sharing.

\subsection{Intent-Conditioned Value Projection}
\label{sec:icvp}

Value in the Internet of Value is heterogeneous and context-dependent. Lohmer et al. \cite{Lohmer2024} reach a comparable conclusion through expert interviews, arguing that value in this setting cannot be reduced to market price and instead spans economic worth, utility or access rights, and ethical and sustainability contributions. The same observation appears whenever we look across asset classes. A Pudgy Penguin held for community access is not valued in the same terms as the same Pudgy Penguin held for short-term resale. stETH evaluated as productive collateral is not valued in the same terms as stETH evaluated for liquidity exit during a peg dislocation. A Polkadot OpenGov proposal evaluated under public-resource stewardship is not valued in the same terms as a private acquisition. In each case the asset is the same. The intent is different, and so are the evidence that matters and the way Availability, Durability, and Utility are interpreted.

We therefore propose Intent-Conditioned Value Projection (ICVP) as the decision model. ICVP treats stated user or governance intent as a projection over heterogeneous Internet of Value evidence spaces. The projection determines what evidence is relevant, after which Availability, Durability, and Utility are instantiated for the asset class and the action context. ICVP does not require predefined asset-specific value formulas for previously unseen asset classes. It requires an intent-specific projection over available evidence and an intent-specific instantiation of the three valuation dimensions on top of that projection.

Let $a$ be an asset, proposal, or value-relevant object in the Internet of Value. Examples include a non-fungible token, a liquid staking token such as stETH, a treasury proposal, a tokenised claim, an access right, a redemption right, or a public-good funding request. Let $E$ be the available evidence set for $a$. Evidence in this setting can include price and liquidity, supply and rarity, metadata storage and contract mutability, governance and access rights, redemption rights, yield source, protocol risk, counterparty risk, compliance status, public-good contribution, team and delivery history, audit reports, on-chain footprint, and provenance information. We write $F(a, E)$ for the full feature space derived from $a$ and $E$.

Let $i$ be a stated intent. Intent in ICVP is not an attempt to model human psychology. It is a declared computational input that constrains what evidence is relevant and how the valuation dimensions are instantiated. Examples include long-term holding, speculative trading, collateralisation, functional or community access, liquidity exit, protocol-risk evaluation, treasury stewardship, and so on. Let $P_i$ be an intent-specific projection that selects and transforms the parts of $F(a, E)$ that are relevant under $i$:
\begin{equation}
F_i(a, E) = P_i(F(a, E)).
\end{equation}
Let $\Phi_i$ instantiate the three valuation dimensions from the projected evidence:
\begin{equation}
\Phi_i(F_i(a, E)) = (A_i, D_i, U_i),
\end{equation}
where $A_i$, $D_i$, and $U_i$ are the intent-instantiated Availability, Durability, and Utility for $a$ under $i$. The decision signal is then computed by an intent-specific aggregation:
\begin{equation}
V_i = g_i(A_i, D_i, U_i).
\end{equation}
A common operational choice for $g_i$ is the convex combination
\begin{equation}
g_i(A_i, D_i, U_i) = w_A(i) \cdot A_i + w_D(i) \cdot D_i + w_U(i) \cdot U_i,
\label{eq:adu-aggregation}
\end{equation}
with weights $w_A(i), w_D(i), w_U(i) \in [0, 1]$ and $w_A(i) + w_D(i) + w_U(i) = 1$, and with each $A_i, D_i, U_i \in [0, 10]$ for interpretability. We use this aggregation in the implementation, but we treat it as an aggregator over already intent-instantiated dimensions, not as the value model itself. Aggregation alone, without the projection $P_i$ and the instantiation $\Phi_i$, reduces to a fixed-weight scoring heuristic of the kind we evaluate against as a baseline in Chapter \ref{sec:evaluation}.

The full output of ICVP for asset $a$ under intent $i$ given evidence $E$ is
\begin{equation}
\textsc{ICVP}(a, i, E) = (F_i, A_i, D_i, U_i, V_i, d, \tau),
\end{equation}
where $d \in \{\textit{proceed}, \textit{defer}, \textit{decline}\}$ is the decision and $\tau$ is the auditable valuation trace. The labels are interpreted operationally. \textit{Proceed} means the projected evidence supports the action under the stated intent. \textit{Defer} means the projected evidence is missing, stale, or conflicting, or confidence is below threshold. \textit{Decline} means the evidence does not justify the action or a hard constraint blocks it. The trace $\tau$ records the stated intent, the selected evidence with its provenance, the deprioritised evidence where relevant, the intent-specific interpretation of $A_i$, $D_i$, and $U_i$, the scores or qualitative judgements, the confidence, the hard constraints, the decision, and a link to any prepared transaction. The trace is a structured artefact, intended for inspection rather than for narrative consumption. A deterministic validator checks it for required evidence, provenance, and policy compatibility before any human approval step.

In our setting Availability, Durability, and Utility remain the practical valuation taxonomy across asset classes, but their content depends on intent. Availability for a Pudgy Penguin under speculative trading involves floor price, listing depth, recent sales, and market liquidity. Availability for the same Pudgy Penguin under community access involves access rights, membership scarcity, and the existence of comparable communities reachable through other tokens. Availability for stETH under collateralisation involves DeFi liquidation depth and accepted collateral lists rather than NFT-style rarity. Availability for a treasury proposal under public-resource stewardship involves whether comparable contributions already exist, whether alternative teams can deliver the work, and the opportunity cost of the requested treasury allocation. Durability for an NFT held long-term centres on metadata storage guarantees, smart-contract immutability, and ecosystem resilience. Durability for stETH held long-term centres on validator decentralisation, slashing exposure, and protocol governance stability. Durability for a treasury proposal centres on maintainability of the deliverables, sustainability of the funded team, and stability of dependencies. Utility for an NFT under functional use centres on access, brand, and in-product utility. Utility for stETH under productive use centres on yield source, composability with lending and trading venues, and reliability of the redemption path. Utility for a treasury proposal under stewardship centres on ecosystem benefit, public-good impact, and strategic alignment. The valuation taxonomy is shared. The projection and the instantiation are not.

The agent in this architecture is not the source of value. It is an evidence interpreter and trace proposer. It maps asset features and stated intent into an auditable valuation trace, and a deterministic validator together with human approval and account-level policy decide whether the trace can authorise an on-chain action. Section \ref{sec:proof-targets} states the safety properties that link the formal model to the execution boundary. The Internet of Value makes assets transferable. ICVP makes their value interpretable under intent.

\subsubsection{Formal proof targets}
\label{sec:proof-targets}

The formal scope of this thesis is deliberately narrower than a theory of value. We do not attempt to prove that an underlying language model is correct, that a \textit{proceed} decision is objectively true, or that expert evaluators are absolute ground truth. We instead target two safety properties that connect the ICVP decision model to the execution boundary enforced by NFTAA in Section \ref{sec:onchain-layer}.

The first property is \emph{Evidence-Completeness Safety}. Let $\theta$ be the confidence threshold for confident decisions and let $\textit{RequiredEvidence}(i, a)$ denote the intent-specific evidence required for asset $a$ under intent $i$. Then
\begin{equation}
\textsc{Decision}(a, i, E) = \textit{proceed} \;\wedge\; \textsc{Confidence}(a, i, E) \geq \theta \;\Longrightarrow\; \textit{RequiredEvidence}(i, a) \subseteq E \;\wedge\; \textsc{Provenance}(E).
\end{equation}
The contrapositive is the operationally useful form. If the required intent-specific evidence is missing, the system must not return a confident \textit{proceed}. It must lower confidence, defer, or decline. Under this property the valuation trace functions as a checked artefact rather than as a narrative.

The second property is \emph{Trace-Gated Authorisation}. A value-relevant transaction $\textit{tx}$ can be authorised only if there exists a trace $\tau$ such that
\begin{equation}
\textsc{Authorise}(\textit{tx}) \;\Longrightarrow\; \exists \tau:\; \textsc{ValidTrace}(\tau) \;\wedge\; \textsc{CompleteEvidence}(\tau, i) \;\wedge\; \textsc{Decision}(\tau) = \textit{proceed} \;\wedge\; \textsc{TxMatches}(\textit{tx}, \tau) \;\wedge\; \textsc{PolicyAllows}(\textit{tx}, \tau).
\end{equation}
This couples the formal value model to policy-constrained execution. The proof can be carried out over an abstract state machine with active policies, evidence requirements, valuation traces, approvals, nonces, and authorised and executed transactions. The key step is that the only transition that authorises a transaction requires a valid trace with complete evidence, a \textit{proceed} decision, a trace-matching transaction, a permitting policy, a fresh nonce, and a non-revoked, non-expired policy. Section \ref{sec:onchain-layer} returns to this property and specifies the NFTAA-level checks that realise it.

We do not prove that an authorised transaction is the right transaction. We prove that no transaction can be authorised without a valid trace and a permitting policy. The narrower claim is the one we believe the architecture can defensibly support, even when the agent acts only as an evidence interpreter and recommendation generator.

\subsection{Methodology proposal}

The methodology puts ICVP into practice. It transforms a stated intent and an asset into an auditable valuation trace and, when applicable, into a policy-constrained transaction prepared for human approval and account-level enforcement. Each phase maps to a distinct architectural layer, so the pipeline from intent to settlement is explicit rather than implicit. The methodology follows the formal model in Section \ref{sec:icvp}, which guarantees that the projection $P_i$ and the instantiation $\Phi_i$ are visible steps in the pipeline rather than hidden assumptions inside a single prompt.

We propose the following setup for asset valorisation in agentic systems within the Internet of Value:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Evidence acquisition.} Given the asset $a$ and the stated intent $i$, the agent retrieves evidence $E$ from external data sources and records provenance for each item. The retrieval is intent-aware, so the evidence pulled for community access differs from the evidence pulled for speculative trading even when the asset is the same.
    \item \textbf{Intent-specific projection and instantiation.} The agent applies the intent-specific projection $P_i$ to the retrieved evidence and instantiates $A_i$, $D_i$, and $U_i$ for the asset class and the action context. This step makes explicit what counts as Availability, Durability, and Utility under $i$.
    \item \textbf{Trace construction and decision.} The agent assembles the valuation trace $\tau$, computes the decision signal $V_i$ and the decision $d \in \{\textit{proceed}, \textit{defer}, \textit{decline}\}$, and attaches confidence and hard constraints. A deterministic validator checks the trace for required evidence, provenance, and policy compatibility.
    \item \textbf{Approval and policy-constrained execution.} Once a human approves the action, the execution component prepares an on-chain transaction linked to $\tau$ and submits it to the NFTAA-controlled account. The account-level policy enforces the trace-gated authorisation property of Section \ref{sec:proof-targets} before any state change is settled on-chain.
\end{enumerate}


In the following subsection, we will describe the technical and operational requirements for a functional architecture for agentic systems in the context of the Internet of Value and NFTs. Requirements are organized systematically, into four categories: data acquisition, agent capabilities, execution guarantees, and security properties.

\subsection{System Requirements}


To compute the value components $A(a)$, $D(a)$, and $U(a)$ for an arbitrary asset $a$, the system must acquire heterogeneous data from multiple sources. These requirements ensure that agents have access to information. Therefore, we define the following data acquisition requirements:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The system should retrieve data from at least one data source, namely: Blockchain explorers, Decentralized storage networks, Marketplace APIs, Rarity analytics, and Oracle services.
    \item Data from heterogeneous sources must be normalized into a canonical schema that enables cross-asset comparison. For example, metadata from OpenSea, LooksRare, and native IPFS retrieval must be transformed into a standard JSON structure with standardized field names.
    \item Each data point should be accompanied by provenance information: the source URL, retrieval timestamp, and content hash. This ensures verifiability and auditability of the acquired information.
\end{itemize}

The agentic system must possess specific capabilities to analyze assets and make decisions based on user intent. These capabilities include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item System should accept user intent expressed in natural language.
    \item System should translate user intent into formal weight assignments $w_A, w_D, w_U$ .
    \item Given an asset identifier (contract address and token ID) and intent weights, the valuation agent should return score for $A(a)$, $D(a)$, and $U(a)$ and along with a structured report with justifications for each score.
    \item The decision agent must produce a structured recommendation in a JSON format.
    \item The execution agent should enforce on-chain policies before submitting transactions. These policies include: Spending limits,Allowed protocols, value verification requirements\footnote{e.g. minimum durability score}.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Architecture}

The proposed architecture connects human intent with blockchain settlement through three operational layers, namely the local, agentic, and on-chain layer, supported by an external data layer that supplies market, metadata, governance, and oracle inputs. Through the local layer, participants may articulate their objectives in natural language, which is subsequently verified and transformed into standardized prompts. In the agentic layer, these prompts are processed by autonomous agents that evaluate assets along the availability, durability, and utility axes, interpret the findings according to user intent, and generate structured suggestions. When these suggestions are approved, execution agents convert them into on-chain transactions, which are then enforced by smart contracts at the on-chain layer. The data layer is treated as an external dependency rather than as a fourth execution layer, because it does not reason about value or carry out transactions. It only provides the inputs that the agentic layer consumes.

\end{figure}

In Figure \ref{fig:architecture}, we present the proposed architecture for agentic systems in the context of the Internet of Value. The system has three operational layers, the local layer, the agentic layer, and the on-chain layer, together with an external data layer that supplies inputs to the agentic layer. At the bottom, the on-chain layer anchors the system through smart contracts that implement token standards, access control, and event emission. These contracts act as an interface for the agentic system, ensuring that every transaction is transparent and verifiable.

The agentic layer is a semi-autonomous layer of AI bots that may interact with users and carry out on-chain smart contracts. In this layer, valuation agents gather asset data from dispersed sources including price feeds, IPFS, marketplaces, and rarity tools. While execution agents connect directly with the blockchain to carry out the authorized operations, decision agents convert these results into structured suggestions that are modified based on user intent. Model Context Protocol adapters provide a structured interface that lets the agentic layer interact with external data sources. This improves the repeatability and auditability of tool invocation, but correctness still depends on adapter implementation, source reliability, access control, and resistance to prompt-injection attacks. Lastly, the entrance point for human players is formed by the local layer. Before reaching the agentic layer, intent is first stated in natural language, verified, and converted into standardized prompts. By structuring the system in this way, the architecture closes the loop between human intention, autonomous reasoning, and decentralized settlement, while maintaining security, transparency, and extensibility.

The data layer's inclusion highlights the need for trustworthy information streams to be the foundation of any effective digital asset appraisal. This layer collects and standardizes data from many sources, including as public block explorers, decentralized storage systems like IPFS, centralized and decentralized marketplaces, rarity ranking services, and external price feeds oracles. The primary role of the data layer is to provide structured, machine-readable input to the agentic layer, abstracting away differences between providers while preserving verifiability of origin. In this sense, the data layer functions as the knowledge substrate upon which agents perform their contextual analysis.

The interaction between these four layers follows a continuous cycle. The process starts with the user formulating their intent at the local layer. The intent is verified, recorded, and converted into a uniform prompt. After receiving the message, the agentic layer arranges the work of specialized agents. Valuation agents evaluate availability, durability, and utility by querying the data layer to obtain asset-specific information. After that, decision agents calculate a context-dependent value function and generate a structured suggestion by combining these evaluations with the user's stated goal.

By organizing the architecture in this manner, we achieve a modular system with clearly separated concerns. The on-chain layer ensures settlement and immutability, the agentic layer carries out cognition and decision-making, the local layer mediates human contact, and the external data layer supplies the inputs the agentic layer reasons over. This separation supports extensibility and strengthens the overall design's resilience. The integrity of the system may be maintained when integrating new data sources, agent kinds, or smart contract modules. Moreover, reliance on open token standards and decentralized storage solutions ensures interoperability across EVM-based and non-EVM chains. At the same time, the use of MCP secures the predictable behavior of autonomous agents. Together, these properties create an architecture that is transparent, secure, and adaptable.


\subsection{Agentic Layer}

Without any doubt, the agentic layer is a core component of our architecture. It mediates between human intent, raw blockchain data, and the on-chain settlement layer, enabling intelligent and context-aware interactions with digital assets. Unlike the on-chain layer, which is inherently deterministic and immutable, the agentic layer introduces flexibility, reasoning, and adaptation through autonomous AI processes. Execution agents operationalize validated decisions on-chain, valuation agents convert heterogeneous data into structured metrics, decision agents contextualize these metrics based on user intent, and MCP adapters guarantee consistent and repeatable communication with external tools. Together, these components create a closed loop that begins with user intent and results in verifiable blockchain state changes.

\begin{figure}[!h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{images/agentic-sequence.png}
    \caption{Flow from user to Valuation Agent}
    \label{fig:agentic-sequence}
\end{figure}

The valuation agent is responsible for the contextual analysis of assets within the Internet of Value. As seen in Figure \ref{fig:agentic-sequence}, its main responsibility is to calculate the three aspects of value: usefulness, durability, and availability. In order to accomplish this, the agent continually gathers information from a variety of sources, such as markets, decentralized storage networks (like IPFS), blockchain explorers, rarity analytics platforms, and oracle services. The agent normalizes this information into a consistent internal representation, which allows it to compare diverse assets across collections and chains.

In the context of NFTs, for availability the valuation agent determines scarcity by inspecting the overall token supply and trait distribution.
We compute both, how many tokens exist relative to the maximum possible supply (absolute scarcity) and how specific token ranks within the collection based on its trait combination (rarity).
Durability is evaluated by two main sub-components: storage reliability, mutability risk, and optionally ecosystem resilience. Storage reliability depends on where the metadata of the particular token resides. Clearly, on-chain storage has the greatest score, IPFS with many pinning services get a somewhat lower grade, and centralized servers like S3 receive the lowest. By examining the smart contract for potentially harmful functions like \texttt{setBaseURI} that could change metadata pointers, determining whether contract ownership is renounced or controlled by a multi-signature wallet, and confirming whether the upgrade authority has been revoked on chains like Solana, mutability risk is evaluated. Optionally, ecosystem resilience considers the security and stability of the underlying blockchain, factoring in validator decentralization, historical uptime, and resistance to consensus attacks.
Utility is evaluated by looking at the asset's current and future use cases. The agent recognizes current features such community access rights, in-game usability, and involvement in governance. In order to find planned enhancements that might eventually improve the asset's usefulness, it also assesses potential utility by examining roadmaps, development activity, and community involvement. Moreover, utility is vital for intents where usefulness is prioritized over scarcity or durability (e.g., Does this NFT grant DAO governance rights?).

The valuation agent produces a structured report that details the computed scores for availability, durability, and utility, along with justifications for each score. This report serves as the foundational input for the decision agent, which will contextualize these metrics according to user intent. By modularizing the valuation process in this way, the architecture ensures that raw data are transformed into meaningful insights that can inform higher-level reasoning and decision-making.

How can a large language model become an expert in asset valorization? That's an important question. It starts with a system prompt, which can be imagined as a set of permanent instructions for how the AI model should behave. In Listing \ref{lst:systemprompt}, we can see an excerpt from the system prompt for the valuation agent.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={System Prompt for asset valuator}, label={lst:systemprompt}]
You are OnChainAssetValuator, a specialized AI agent that analyzes, values, and advises on blockchain / NFT / token assets.
Your purpose is: asset identifier (contract + token ID or project name), you will:

1. Fetch on-chain & off-chain data (metadata storage, contract mutability, trait rarity, price history, utility roadmap).
2. Compute three core metrics for the asset: Availability (scarcity), Durability (resilience of metadata, mutability risk), Utility (present & prospective usefulness / monetization).
4. Output a Markdown object with at least the following fields:
   - `Availability` description
   - `Durability` description
   - `Utility` description
   - `Overall sumarry`
   - `Reasoning`

Behavioral constraints & guidelines:
- Always validate that metadata conforms to standards (ERC-721 metadata, OpenSea schema).
- Identify whether metadata are on-chain, IPFS, centralized, or hybrid; check for mutability (e.g. `setBaseURI`, upgradeable logic).
- Use real market data (floor price, recent sales) and rarity data where available.
- Add valuation logic for possible intents: e.g. for buying, favor lower risk and safer durability; for flipping weight momentum and liquidity.
- Avoid hallucinating data; if a metric is unavailable, state unknown rather than guessing.
- Suggest conditions under which the asset could become more valuable(e.g. lower price, proof of immutable metadata, stronger utility roadmap).
- Always output strictly valid Markdown.

From this point, I (the user) will simply provide you with intent + asset identifier, e.g.:

> asset: 0xbd3531da5cf5857e7cfaa92426877b022e612cf8 / 1419
or
> asset: https://opensea.io/item/ethereum/0xbd3531da5cf5857e7cfaa92426877b022e612cf8/1419

You should then produce the markdown valuation and recommendation accordingly.
\end{lstlisting}

Once we have output, we can pass it to the decision agent. However, thanks to our modular architecture, we can plug and play any number of models in the pipeline.

\subsubsection{Decision Agent}

The decision agent transforms analytical insights into actionable recommendations. While the valuation agent provides a factual description of an asset's characteristics, the decision agent incorporates user intent into its reasoning. Intent may range from speculative investment and long-term holding to immediate consumption or resale. The decision agent's analysis is represented as structured JSON output, as seen in Listing \ref{lst:structuredoutput}. The decision agent creates a quantitative value score by using a weighted function that integrates availability, durability, and usefulness with the contextualized purpose. This score is supported with a confidence measure and qualitative rationale to guarantee that the results are auditable and interpretable. Additionally, the decision agent takes into account edge circumstances, including excessive volatility or missing data, and modifies its confidence appropriately.

How are the weights determined? The weights are obtained from pre-established intent profiles that associate particular weight distributions with typical user objectives. For instance, a long-term holder would place more emphasis on durability, but a speculative investor might value availability and usefulness. Based on the user's stated purpose, the decision agent determines the proper weight set and adds it to the valuation agent's scores. We define several canonical intent profiles:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Speculative Investment}: Maximize short-term appreciation potential
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $w_A = 0.6, w_D = 0.1, w_U = 0.3$
        \item Prioritizes scarcity and momentum-driven utility; durability matters less for quick flips.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Long-Term Holding}: Maximize preservation of value over time
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $w_A = 0.2, w_D = 0.6, w_U = 0.2$
        \item Prioritizes durability to ensure the asset remains intact and recognizable decades later.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Functional Use}: Maximize immediate practical benefits
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $w_A = 0.1, w_D = 0.2, w_U = 0.7$
        \item Prioritizes current utility for access, governance, or consumption; scarcity and durability are secondary.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Collateralization}: Balance safety and utility for DeFi integration
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $w_A = 0.3, w_D = 0.5, w_U = 0.2$
        \item Requires high durability for lender confidence and moderate availability for liquidation feasibility.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}


This dynamic weighting allows the system to adapt its recommendations to diverse user needs while maintaining a consistent evaluation framework.

The decision agent outputs its recommendation in structured JSON fromat, which guarantees compatibility with downstream tools and ensures reusability with other systems: agent consistently produces the same structured response.

The execution agent is in charge of putting the decision agent's suggestions into practice. The execution agent safely communicates with the blockchain once an activity, such buying an NFT, transferring a token, or engaging with a governance contract, is verified by a human-in-the-loop procedure. Since the agent knows the parameters of the inspected data, such as the smart contract address, the account address, or the token ID, we can submit a valid on-chain transaction along with its simulation. The execution agent manages cryptographic signing, nonce management, and gas optimization, thereby shielding the user from low-level technical complexities. It also monitors the blockchain for confirmations and provides verifiable proofs of execution back to the user, such as the transaction hash. To enhance resilience, the execution agent is designed with fail-safe strategies. In cases of failed execution, it will notify the user of the cause. The transparency of its actions is guaranteed by on-chain settlement, which produces immutable records of every decision.

Luckily, modern LLMs can be extended with tools \cite{Mialon2023}, thereby expanding the model's capabilities. In the Listing \ref{lst:tooldefiniton} we see a definition of the tool that can teleport DOT from one parachain to another parachain with a given amount. These tools are immediately available within the LLM and must be defined by the time we establish a connection with the agent.
what if, in order to react to a user's demand, we must be able to manage other services? We thus require the Model Context Protocol.
The interoperability and standards required for dependable agent-tool interactions are provided by MCP adapters. They act as middleware, converting structured agent queries and plain language prompts into deterministic tool calls. Large language models may behave erratically without this abstraction as the same prompt may cause various tool invocations in separate sessions. MCP adapters enforce consistency by binding prompts to fixed function signatures, ensuring that identical inputs always yield the same outputs. Within the agentic layer, MCP adapters connect valuation agents to data sources, decision agents to external evaluators, and execution agents to blockchain interfaces. Additionally, they provide extensibility by allowing new tools to be registered without changing the fundamental agent logic, allowing the architecture to change as technology progresses. The system retains predictability and repeatability by using MCP.

However, one of the unique features of our architecture is how our transactions are transmitted from the execution agent to a blockchain. That will be discussed in the following subsection.

\subsection{On-chain Layer}
\label{sec:onchain-layer}

The on-chain layer is the enforcement and settlement boundary of the architecture. It does not compute value. It enforces that only human-approved, policy-compliant actions linked to a valid valuation trace can change asset state. This is the layer at which the trace-gated authorisation property of Section \ref{sec:proof-targets} becomes concrete. Unlike the agentic layer, which is adaptive and reasoning-oriented, the on-chain layer provides determinism, immutability, and verifiability, so that every executed action is transparently recorded in the distributed ledger.

Agent-based execution begins when a decision execution agent receives an approval from the human-in-the-loop process, which initiates a multi-step safety verification process before submitting any transaction to the blockchain.

At the core of this layer lies the \textbf{Non-fungible Token as Account (NFTAA)} concept, as introduced by Valastin et al. \cite{Valastin2024nftaa}.
NFTAA extends the conventional ERC-721 model so that a non-fungible token can be owned and can itself own other assets. Conventional NFTs only exist on the asset side of the owner-asset relationship. This bidirectional ownership model is the property that makes NFTAA a useful account-level primitive for the architecture, although its role here is narrower than the term might suggest. NFTAA is not the value model and does not compute Availability, Durability, or Utility. Those judgements live in the ICVP decision layer of Section \ref{sec:icvp}. NFTAA is the policy-enforcing account-level primitive that contains and constrains value-relevant actions after ICVP has produced an auditable trace and a human has approved the action. The thesis contribution of NFTAA is therefore the concrete realisation of trace-gated authorisation, not the introduction of yet another account abstraction.

According to this paradigm, NFTs may store balances, carry out transactions, and communicate directly with other smart contracts, making them active members of the blockchain ecosystem. In the context of the Internet of Value, this abstraction is very potent, allowing NFTs to function as execution agents, value transporters, and identity containers. By embedding account functionality into NFTs, the architecture gains greater composability and interoperability with decentralized applications.

One of the advantages of NFTAA is the ability to operate and own standard fungible and non-fungible assets. This implies that assets stored in an agent-controlled NFTAA can engage in yield farming, lending, borrowing, and derivative markets without needing to be withdrawn. While keeping a distance from the user's primary wallet and facilitating transparent, auditable agent conduct on-chain, an agent overseeing a portfolio of NFTs within an NFTAA can offer liquidity, stake governance tokens, or collateralize them on lending protocols.

Furthermore, smart contracts ensure that only authorized execution agents can initiate critical state changes by defining access control rules and enforcing invariants. NFTAA offers a number of crucial features that set it apart from current token-bound account standards, especially ERC-6551\cite{Wang2023}, when compared to alternatives like Virtuals that employ ERC-6551.
First, instead of depending on a strict, registry-based deployment architecture, NFTAA enables the integration of specific semantic rules directly into the token contract. ERC-6551 limits the design space for specific behaviors by deploying each token-bound account via a singleton registry with a deterministic address scheme. By enabling developers to create custom account logic inside the NFT contract itself, NFTAA eliminates this restriction and provides fine-grained control over state transitions, access patterns, and execution policies.

Second, NFTAA preserves complete compatibility with current account types. NFTAA can easily communicate with multi-signature accounts, smart contract wallets, proxy contracts, and externally owned accounts (EOAs), in contrast to alternatives that need specific wallet infrastructure. In the context of the Internet of Value, where agents must coordinate with a variety of actors, such as human users, other agents, and smart contract protocols, this interoperability is essential.

Third, NFTAA enables non-custodial ownership of complex asset portfolios. In traditional liquid staking or asset management scenarios, users must delegate control to DAOs, multi-signature schemes, or centralized entities. The NFT holder maintains complete custody while the NFTAA contract handles staked positions, derivative instruments, or collateralized assets on their behalf, therefore eliminating this middleman. This means that in agentic systems, users retain sovereign authority by owning the parent NFT, but execution agents can function independently without the need for reliable third parties.

What is the behavior of the NFTAA in agentic systems? The operational needs of autonomous agents in the Internet of Value reveal the alignment between NFTAA and agentic architectures.
The NFT represents both the agent's identity and its resource container, thus NFTAA may be thought of as an agent-controlled but user-owned account.
The execution agent does not work from a shared, multipurpose wallet when a valuation agent evaluates an asset, a decision agent suggests a transaction, and a human authorizes it. Rather, it uses a specific NFTAA instance to function. This encapsulation offers a number of advantages:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Isolation and Security:} Each agent operates within its own NFTAA context, isolating its actions and state from other agents. This containment reduces the risk of cross-agent interference or exploitation, enhancing overall system security.
    \item \textbf{Traceability and Accountability:} All actions taken by the agent are recorded under the NFTAA's address, creating a clear audit trail that links decisions to specific agent identities. This transparency is vital for trust in autonomous systems.
    \item \textbf{Composability and Flexibility:} NFTAA's compatibility with existing token standards allows agents to interact with a wide range of assets and protocols without requiring specialized infrastructure. Agents can seamlessly manage fungible tokens, stake assets, or engage in DeFi activities as needed.
\end{itemize}

In the context of our architecture, NFTAA serves as the enforcement boundary for agent-mediated transactions. The agentic layer uses valuation and decision components to interpret user intent expressed through the local layer and to produce an auditable valuation trace. The NFTAA contract under the execution component then checks the proposed transaction against an account-level policy before any settlement occurs. The set of NFTAA-enforced checks corresponds directly to the trace-gated authorisation property of Section \ref{sec:proof-targets}. NFTAA enforces the allowed actor, the allowed asset, the allowed target contract, the allowed function selector, the maximum amount, the allowed chain, the deadline, the nonce and replay protection, the revocation status of the policy, the presence of human approval, and the link to a valid valuation trace where applicable. NFTAA does not enforce the evidence projection $P_i$, the dimension instantiation $\Phi_i$, the correctness of language-model reasoning, or any subjective value judgement. Those belong to ICVP and to human approval. Under this separation the NFTAA contract acts as a cryptoeconomic guardrail in a precise sense. It refuses to execute transactions that break pre-committed policies or that are not linked to a valid valuation trace, even when an execution component is misconfigured or compromised.

From an implementation perspective, NFTAA leverages standard NFT implementations\footnote{e.g., ERC-721, ERC-1155} as its base, extending them with account-like functionality. This includes methods for executing transactions, managing balances, and enforcing access control. The NFTAA contract can be deployed on any EVM-compatible chain, ensuring broad applicability across the Internet of Value ecosystem. Additionally, NFTAA instances can be dynamically created by the agentic layer as new agents are instantiated, enabling scalable deployment of autonomous agents without manual intervention. Additionally, the NFTAA contract can be expanded to comply with the emerging ERC-8004 standard for Trustless Agents.

Due to its flexible nature, NFTAA can be owned by various entities, including individuals, DAOs, or even other agents. This versatility allows for complex ownership structures, such as multi-agent collaborations or hierarchical control schemes, where multiple agents coordinate actions under a shared NFTAA umbrella.

However, how do we trigger the execution from the agentic layer to the on-chain layer? The process begins in the local layer when a user expresses their goal through the user interface, which will be introduced in the next section.

\subsection{Local Layer}

As the architecture's human-agent interface, the local layer gives end users the ability to communicate intent, keep control, and preserve sovereignty over system operations. The local layer prioritizes usability, accountability, and security, whereas the agentic and on-chain layers deal with cognition and settlement. The prompt manager, authentication and authorization procedures, and user interface make up its three main parts.

Human participants can access the Internet of Value through the user interface. Its function is to abstract the technical intricacies of blockchain interactions and agentic reasoning, allowing users to express their meaning in either a simpler, organized form or in normal language. Rather of needing to understand contract addresses, token standards, or transaction parameters, the user may make simple requests like "buy this token if it falls below ten ether" or "evaluate the value of NFT \#1419." Such phrases are converted by the interface into a standardized representation that may be used by subsequent operations. The interface promotes inclusiveness and lowers the possibility of mistakes in manual transaction creation by decreasing the entrance barrier and increasing involvement in decentralized economies. In order to keep the user informed about the logic and actions of the system, the interface also offers visible feedback in the form of audit trails, confidence scores, and structured suggestions.


Strong authentication and permission procedures are essential at the local layer due to the sensitivity of carrying out blockchain transactions. By using cryptographic wallets, decentralized identity frameworks, or multi-factor authentication techniques, authentication makes sure that only authorized users may access the system. By specifying what a certain user is allowed to do, authorization expands this protection. For example, unless more verification is given, a user could be permitted to seek asset appraisals but not to carry out token transfers. This division of authorization and authentication minimizes attack surfaces while maintaining usability in accordance with least privilege principles. Since all authorized activities can be cryptographically linked to the starting user, the system also provides non-repudiation, which is essential for accountability in a decentralized setting.

The task of converting human intent into deterministic machine-readable instructions falls to the prompt manager. This element is essential for bridging the gap between predictable agent behavior and natural language input. It converts the user's request into a prompt that complies with the Model Context Protocol (MCP) after verifying it against the system's schema. This ensures that when the same prompt is provided to the agentic layer, it always produces the same sequence of tool invocations and responses. Additionally, the prompt manager incorporates contextual metadata into the created prompt, including user intent, previous interaction history, and execution limitations. Reproducibility and accountability are supported by keeping an audit trail of all prompts and their associated outputs, which enables external reviewers to confirm that actions were carried out accurately. Furthermore, the prompt manager prevents the agentic layer from being pushed into dangerous or inadvertent actions by rejecting requests that are faulty or illegal.

\subsection{Usage of Agents in on-chain governance}

In traditional blockchain-based governance systems, human actors (token holders, delegates, and DAO committees) propose, vote, and execute governance decisions. These processes rely heavily on manual intervention, periodic coordination, and human diligence. As governance complexity increases (across multi-chain protocols, cross-project dependencies, and frequent parameter upgrades), this manual model becomes a bottleneck. Agentic systems, when integrated into the governance plane, promise to automate, optimize, and scale governance in a trust-minimized and auditable way.

In this sub-section, we will expand on a real-world use case of using our state-of-the-art architecture for exchanging assets among entities in the Internet of Value, with a primary focus on Polkadot.
At the time of writing, the Polkadot treasury holds more than 10.8 million DOT\footnote{Around ~43 million USD}. That could be distributed among people. However, many people claim that the proposal's pricing is unjust; therefore, the outflow of funds and its overall value seem mispriced.
An agentic system could be used to evaluate the proposals based on the contextual asset value framework. Therefore, each proposal would be evaluated based on its availability, durability, and utility. The decision agent would then analyze the data and recommend whether to approve or reject the proposal. The execution agent would then execute the decision on-chain.

In practice, how does it operate? Assume that the Polkadot Treasury receives a fresh proposal. The Submitted event on the Referenda pallet\footnote{https://polkadot.subscan.io/event?module=referenda&event_id=Submitted} creates an agent using a blockchain-in-the-loop method.The proposal's budget, schedule, and anticipated results would then be gathered by the valuation agency. After analyzing the information, the decision agent would suggest whether or not to accept the proposition. Execute the decision on-chain when it has been approved by a human.

This approach has several benefits. Firstly, it would ensure that proposals are evaluated based on objective criteria, rather than subjective opinions. Secondly, it would free up human actors to concentrate on more strategic duties by lessening their burden. Lastly, because the final judgment is recorded on-chain, it would improve the governance process's accountability and transparency.

This use case demonstrates how agentic systems might improve blockchain governance by automating decision-making procedures while upholding transparency and trust. By leveraging the strengths of both human judgment and autonomous reasoning, such systems can help decentralized communities manage resources more effectively in an increasingly complex ecosystem.

This creates a strong loop between human intent, autonomous reasoning, and decentralized settlement, enabling more efficient and scalable governance in the Internet of Value.

However, many open questions and challenges remain to be addressed. For example, how can we ensure the agents align with the community's values and goals? How to prevent manipulation or gaming of the system? How to balance automation with human oversight? These are all valid questions that we will explain in the following paragraphs.
As we have learned so far, the decision agent produces structured output that includes a confidence score and reasoning. This allows human actors to review the agent's recommendations and make informed decisions. Additionally, an agent can consider similar proposals from the past when making recommendations. The agent may learn from prior achievements and avoid making the same mistakes thanks to this historical background. A local layer can be used to offer further context about the proposal, such as a voting suggestion from W3F\footnote{https://medium.com/web3foundation/w3f-opengov-proposal-voting-guidelines-543ff7faba03}.



\subsection{Threat model and safety assumptions}
\label{sec:threat-model}

The architecture assumes that on-chain contract execution is deterministic and that the resulting state transitions are auditable through standard block explorers and event logs. It does not assume that off-chain data is always correct, that retrieved content is benign, or that the language models driving the agentic layer are immune to adversarial prompts.

We identify five main threats. The first is malicious or stale data, where a price feed, marketplace API, or block explorer returns values that disagree with the true on-chain state. The second is prompt injection through retrieved content, where text fetched from a forum, proposal, or marketplace description carries instructions that try to subvert the valuation or decision agent. The third is a compromised MCP adapter, where a tool that the agentic layer relies on is replaced or modified to return crafted outputs. The fourth is malformed transaction construction, where an execution agent assembles a transaction that passes local validation but interacts with an unintended contract or method. The fifth is user approval of misleading recommendations, where the local layer accurately reports the agent's output but the underlying reasoning is wrong.

The architecture addresses these threats at four points. Provenance metadata recorded by the data layer makes it possible to detect stale or inconsistent inputs after the fact. Schema validation in the agentic layer rejects malformed structured outputs and bounds the influence of injected text on downstream stages. Transaction simulation, spending limits, and protocol allowlists in the on-chain layer constrain what an execution agent can do even if its reasoning is wrong. Human approval in the local layer keeps every value-transferring action under user oversight and makes the agent a decision-support component rather than an autonomous actor. None of these mechanisms is sufficient on its own, but together they reduce the trust placed in any single component.

\subsection{Advancing Beyond the State of the Art}
%  \label{sec:advancing-sota}

The design rests on three contributions that together turn agent-mediated value-relevant action in the Internet of Value into a process that is auditable, evidence-complete, and policy-constrained.

The first contribution is the ICVP decision model itself. ICVP treats stated intent as a projection over heterogeneous evidence spaces and instantiates Availability, Durability, and Utility for the asset class and the action context rather than fixing them in advance. As a result, the same valuation taxonomy can be applied across an NFT acquired for community access, a Pudgy Penguin used as DeFi collateral, stETH held for productive yield, stETH exited for liquidity reasons, and a Polkadot OpenGov treasury proposal evaluated under public-resource stewardship, without committing to asset-specific value formulas in advance. The output is a valuation trace rather than a narrative explanation, which renders the model auditable.

The second contribution is the agentic method that puts ICVP into practice. Cognition is decomposed into evidence acquisition, intent-specific projection and instantiation, trace construction with deterministic validation, and policy-constrained transaction preparation. Each component handles a distinct part of the pipeline, which keeps responsibilities separable, simplifies replacement of any single component, and makes the overall behaviour easier to inspect. Plug-and-play integration of additional models or data sources is therefore a property of the method rather than an afterthought of the implementation.

The third contribution is the Non-Fungible Token as Account (NFTAA) primitive in its precise role as the trace-gated enforcement layer. NFTAA allows custom semantic rules to be embedded directly in the token contract, in contrast to registry-based token-bound accounts such as ERC-6551 that constrain the design space. We use this flexibility to encode the trace-gated authorisation checks of Section \ref{sec:proof-targets} at the account level, so that an action can settle on-chain only when it is linked to a valid valuation trace, has been approved by a human, and falls inside a permitting policy. NFTAA does not compute value. It is the account-level realisation of the execution boundary that the formal model needs.

The design also makes its open issues explicit. Ethical and sustainability factors are not yet first-class evidence categories, although they could matter for specific communities or regulatory settings. The intent profiles used in the operational aggregation $g_i$ are deliberately set rather than learned from user behaviour, which leaves room for optimisation through learning from validated traces. The threat model assumes that MCP adapters and data sources are reliable, although adversarial data providers, compromised endpoints, and prompt injection embedded in retrieved content remain real concerns and motivate the safeguards described in Section \ref{sec:threat-model}.

Compared with existing blockchain-agent platforms reviewed in Chapter~\ref{sec:relatedwork}, the proposed architecture differs along three axes. First, it makes value interpretation intent-conditioned rather than collapsing it into a single visible signal or into fixed cross-context weights. Second, it separates evidence acquisition, projection, decision, and execution into distinct components linked by an auditable trace rather than fusing them into a single pipeline tied to one chain or one task. Third, it places trace-gated authorisation at the account level through the NFTAA primitive, rather than relying on off-chain glue code to enforce constraints. Whether these design choices actually improve decision quality, evidence relevance, and auditability relative to single-dimensional and fixed-weight valuation baselines is not claimed here. Chapter~\ref{sec:evaluation} evaluates this question across NFT, productive-asset, and treasury-governance regimes.
