\section{Introduction}

In the 1990s, many forward-thinking people thought that humanity would drive autonomous flying cars and live in vertical ultra-modern skyscrapers in 30 years \cite{Mcrae1994, Berry1996}.
Thirty years later (in the present), there is much research regarding autonomous cars \cite{Faisal2019} and sustainable architecture \cite{Sassi2006}, but non-of the past thinkers were thinking about the internet.
The internet allows people to connect with themselves anywhere around the world.
In 1997 Jeff Bezos created a bookshop on the internet - Amazon, almost nobody believed in him, and people thought the internet was just another bubble. 
Amazon can rapidly deliver physical and digital assets to our doorstep using customer-obsessed business. Therefore they bring value to every part of life \cite{Mellahi2000, Kotha1998}.

The same paradigm shift happened when Satoshi Nakamoto released the Bitcoin whitepaper - the most cited academic paper in the area of blockchain.
Advanced cryptographical methods backed the idea of having fully digital, peer-to-peer electronic money. With limited supply and a probability of around zero percent that the supply will ever change, many people mark it as digital gold \cite{Taskinsoy2021, Uddin2020}. Nevertheless, still, people are skeptical \cite{Nakamoto}.  

As we may agree, the internet has 3 phases related to assets:
First, it was to place real-world assets, such as cars or books, into the virtual world.
The second phase is to enhance these assets with the physically impossible features in the digital world.
Using augmented reality, we can rent a piece of virtual furniture from IKEA and decorate our house in real life without moving our bodies to the store or imagining how it would look on this side of the flat. Just-in-time.
The third and latest phase is the digital-first assets. First, indeed, internet native assets are cryptocurrencies. Unlike traditional assets that derive value from physical scarcity or institutional backing, cryptocurrencies rely on cryptographic guarantees, fixed supply schedules, and decentralized consensus for their value proposition. This fundamental shift challenges conventional notions of money: without a central authority guaranteeing redemption and with high price volatility, adoption requires trust in mathematical proofs rather than institutional promises \cite{Taskinsoy2021}.

Moving towards the internet native world, humans are not the only entities creating and sharing value in the virtual space. With advancements in large language models, AI agents can mimic the same behaviour. Therefore, we need a new virtual world in which humans and AI agents can co-exist in parallel. The usual coined term for virtual worlds is Metaverse, first used in the sci-fi novel Snow Crash in 1992 \cite{Stephenson1992}.
At the time of writing, the author predicted the use of electronic currency that was fast, secure, and easy to use. In contrast to traditional "hyperinflated" currencies, digital currency has become a better monetary asset for storing and exchanging value.
Nevertheless, why is digital currency so valuable? The answer cannot be condensed into a single statement.
The most significant advantage of electronic currency is the ability to build a set of components that can later be reused and composed into other parts of the system.
Why do we mention Snow Crash in the topic of the Internet of Value? Snow Crash predicted a world where information, reputation, and access serve as a currency, which is precisely what the Internet of Value infrastructure is building toward. Going back to the novel, we have seen the first use case of the Internet of Value: a marketplace for buying and selling information \cite{Cao2022, Kraus2022}.
Non-fungible data can have different values and be used in various ways to trade for tokens with monetary value, without traditional intermediaries.

However, while centralized databases can technically store and transfer digital assets, they cannot provide the decentralized trust guarantees required for a truly open, peer-to-peer value network. A centralized server remains under the control of a single operator who can unilaterally modify records, censor transactions, or become a single point of failure \cite{Chowdhury2018}.
Using blockchain as a source of truth, we can create a decentralized system whose data are mutable only via the joint agreement of the participants in the network \cite{Zheng2017}. 
It means that every modification to the data is recorded in an append-only list of transactions that can be reversely read, and therefore building a business intelligence around that is much more straightforward \cite{Wang2022}.

This thesis aims to use the term Internet of Value in the context of decentralized ledger technology and cryptocurrencies from the technological and architectural point of view.
Moreover, we propose a layered agentic architecture that enables autonomous agents to interpret, evaluate, and execute value transfers on behalf of human users, thereby bridging human intent with on-chain execution \cite{Xi2023}.

From the technological perspective, this work is highly oriented toward the Substrate framework and its architecture, focusing on the Polkadot and PolkaVM (RISC-V EVM).
However, we can compare many state-of-the-art blockchain technologies (EVM, Cosmos, Avalanche, Solana).

This work is organized as follows: In Chapter \ref{sec:blockchain}, we describe blockchain technology's fundamentals and advanced concepts.
Chapter \ref{sec:analysis} shows the importance of the Internet of Value, its problems, and a possible solution.
The detailed aims of this work are described in Chapter \ref{sec:aims}.
The architecture and design of our state-of-the-art solution are outlined in Chapter \ref{sec:design} with a comparison with related work. 
Finally, we discuss the results, conclusions, and future work in Chapter \ref{sec:conclusions}.
